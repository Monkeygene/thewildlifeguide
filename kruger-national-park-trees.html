Trees of Kruger National Park

by The Wildlife Guide

Kruger National Park’s landscapes are defined by an extraordinary tapestry of trees, each one playing its part in the ancient rhythms of the bush. From the fever trees that glow chartreuse beside hidden pans, to the gnarled leadwoods whose silver bark tells of centuries gone by, these giants offer refuge and sustenance to countless creatures. In the dry season, the mopane woodlands crackle with life as elephants strip their leaves, while winter mornings find marula fruits scattered like amber jewels beneath the branches. Wander beneath these canopies, and you’ll discover not just shade, but the living history of the African veld.

Baobab (Adansonia digitata)

The baobab tree, known as the “Tree of Life,” towers with a swollen trunk that stores water. Ancient and iconic, it provides shelter, food, and legend, shaping both the savanna ecosystem and African cultural traditions.

Buffalo Thorn (Ziziphus mucronata)

The buffalo thorn, with its zigzag branches and paired thorns one straight, one hooked symbolizes life’s journey. Valued for shade, resilience, and cultural meaning, it offers fruit for wildlife and protection in Kruger’s diverse bushveld landscapes.

False Marula (Lannea schweinfurthii)

The false marula resembles its famous cousin but lacks the sweet fruit. Found in woodlands and savannas, it’s prized for its hardy nature, rough bark, and ability to support browsing wildlife, quietly shaping Kruger’s natural tapestry.

Fever Tree (Vachellia xanthophloea)

The fever tree stands out with its eerie yellow-green bark, thriving near water in Kruger’s lowveld. Once wrongly blamed for malaria, it now supports rich biodiversity and adds striking beauty to the park’s riverine landscapes.

Green Thorn (Senegalia senegal)

The green thorn, a hardy acacia of Kruger’s arid zones, bears pale bark and fine leaflets. Its pods feed wildlife, and its sap once yielded gum arabic. Tough and tenacious, it thrives where few trees dare.

Jackalberry (Diospyros mespiliformis)

The jackalberry tree, tall and evergreen, thrives on Kruger’s riverbanks. Its sweet, nutritious fruit attracts elephants, birds, and, yes—jackals. With dense wood and deep roots, it symbolizes strength, stability, and sustains life through every dry season.

Knob Thorn (Senegalia nigrescens)

The knob thorn rises boldly in Kruger’s savannas, its bark studded with tough, rounded knobs. Blooming with creamy flowers in spring, it feeds giraffes and bees alike, playing a vital role in the dry bush ecosystem.

Lala Palm (Hyphaene coriacea)

The lala palm, Kruger’s only indigenous palm, fans out along eastern riverbanks. Its fruit feeds baboons and birds, while its fibrous leaves serve traditional weaving. Resilient and elegant, it brings a tropical flair to the savanna.

Leadwood (Combretum imberbe)

The leadwood stands ancient and unyielding, its pale, ghostly bark marking Kruger’s landscapes. With wood so dense it sinks in water, it endures centuries—even after death—offering shelter, perch, and silent testimony to time’s passing.

Magic Guarri (Euclea divinorum)

The magic guarri, a hardy shrub or small tree, thrives in Kruger’s varied soils. Its glossy leaves and small berries feed birds and mammals, while its name hints at traditional uses in cleansing, healing, and quiet superstition.

Marula (Sclerocarya birrea)

The marula tree, beloved in Kruger, bursts with juicy fruit that delights elephants, monkeys, and humans alike. Revered for its nutritional and cultural value, it fuels legends, brews, and life itself across the sunlit African savanna.

Mopane (Colophospermum mopane)

Mopane trees blanket Kruger’s northern plains with butterfly-shaped leaves and coppery hues. Adapted to heat and drought, they nourish elephants and mopane worms alike, forming a resilient, life-sustaining woodland that hums with ecological importance and seasonal transformation.

Red Bushwillow (Combretum apiculatum)

The red bushwillow, common in Kruger’s mixed woodlands, flaunts fiery autumn leaves and winged seed pods. Its dense wood resists fire, while its foliage feeds browsers, making it a hardy, vital presence in the park’s wild mosaic.

Sausage Tree (Kigelia africana)

The sausage tree dazzles with bold red flowers and hefty, sausage-shaped fruit dangling like ornaments. Found near Kruger’s rivers, it nourishes wildlife and inspires folklore, blending beauty, utility, and mystique beneath its wide, leafy canopy.

Silver Cluster Leaf (Terminalia sericea)

The silver cluster leaf glows in Kruger’s sunlight, its velvety, silvery leaves shimmering in the breeze. Thriving in sandy soils, it provides shelter and browse, forming thickets that support diverse life and whisper of resilience in the bush.

Tamboti (Spirostachys africana)

The tamboti tree, with its graceful form and spicy-sweet scent, hides a toxic secret—its milky sap can be harmful. Found in Kruger’s woodlands, it supports wildlife yet challenges humans, balancing beauty with a touch of danger.

Umbrella Thorn (Vachellia tortilis)

The umbrella thorn, with its flat, spreading crown, defines Kruger’s classic savanna silhouette. Armed with long white thorns, it shelters wildlife and offers nutritious pods, embodying both protection and sustenance in the park’s sun-drenched open landscapes.

Velvet Raisin (Grewia flavescens)

The velvet raisin, a modest shrub or small tree, dots Kruger’s bushveld with rough bark and soft leaves. Its small, sweet fruits attract birds and antelope, quietly enriching the ecosystem with nourishment, cover, and seasonal charm.

Weeping Boer‑bean (Schotia brachypetala)

The weeping boer-bean bursts into crimson bloom, dripping nectar that draws birds, bees, and monkeys. Found in Kruger’s rocky soils, it offers shade and sustenance, its vivid flowers and sturdy form making it a beloved seasonal spectacle.

Wild Teak (Pterocarpus angolensis)

Wild teak, with its flaking bark and golden autumn leaves, stands tall in Kruger’s woodlands. Valued for its strong, beautiful timber, it also feeds browsers and brightens the bush, blending practicality with seasonal bursts of color.
