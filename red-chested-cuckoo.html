Red-Chested Cuckoo

(Cuculus solitarius)

The Red-Chested Cuckoo (Cuculus solitarius) is a medium-sized bird known for its distinct call, often heard before it is seen. It features a sleek body with a red-brown chest, contrasting with its grayish upperparts. This species is widely distributed across sub-Saharan Africa and is known for its migratory habits. It prefers dense forests and woodlands, where it feeds mainly on caterpillars and insects.

Red-Chested Cuckoo Identification

The Red-Chested Cuckoo is a medium-sized bird with a distinct chestnut-red underside and a grayish upper body. It has a slender, slightly curved bill, long tail, and striking yellow eye-ring. In flight, it displays a rapid wingbeat and a graceful, streamlined body. It is often recognized by its characteristic call, a repeated "piet-my-vrou" sound.

Distribution of the Red-Chested Cuckoo

Countries

The Red-Chested Cuckoo is found in various regions across sub-Saharan Africa, including South Africa, Zimbabwe, Botswana, Namibia, Zambia, Malawi, Tanzania, Kenya, and Uganda. It prefers forested and woodland habitats.

Migration

This species is a partial migrant, with populations in southern Africa moving northwards during the austral winter. Some individuals remain in their breeding territories year-round, while others migrate to central and eastern Africa in search of more favorable conditions.

Red-Chested Cuckoo Habitat

The Red-Chested Cuckoo prefers dense forests, woodlands, and riverine vegetation. It is commonly found in subtropical and tropical regions, where it thrives in areas with plenty of tree cover and a rich insect population.

Feeding & Diet of the Red-Chested Cuckoo

The Red-Chested Cuckoo primarily feeds on insects, with caterpillars being its favorite prey. It also consumes beetles, grasshoppers, and other small invertebrates. This bird forages among trees and shrubs, using its keen eyesight to locate prey before swooping down to catch it.

Plumage of the Red-Chested Cuckoo

Adult

Adult males have a rich chestnut-red breast and belly, with a slate-gray head, back, and wings. Their eye-ring is a striking yellow, and they have a sleek, slightly curved beak. Females resemble males but may have a slightly paler coloration.

Juvenile

Juveniles are duller in coloration, with a more mottled chestnut and gray pattern. They often have a scaly appearance due to pale feather edges and lack the pronounced yellow eye-ring seen in adults. Immature birds gradually acquire the adult coloration over time.

Behaviour of the Red-Chested Cuckoo

Social

The Red-Chested Cuckoo is generally solitary and elusive, often remaining hidden in dense foliage. It is more often heard than seen, thanks to its distinctive call. Unlike many other birds, it does not form flocks but maintains an independent existence.

Breeding

This species is a brood parasite, meaning it does not build its own nest but instead lays eggs in the nests of other bird species. The most common hosts include robin-chats and thrushes. The cuckoo chick hatches earlier than the host’s young and often removes the other eggs or chicks from the nest, ensuring it receives all parental care.

Call

The Red-Chested Cuckoo is well known for its distinctive call, often described as a repetitive and melodious “piet-my-vrou” sound. This call is a key identifier of the species, particularly during the breeding season when males vocalize frequently to establish territory and attract mates.
